THE SOVEREIGN RESTORATION DOCTRINE

By Chief Mathias El (First‑Person Formal Doctrine)

Preamble

I establish this doctrine to clearly state the historical pattern that affected my people and to define the framework I use to restore identity, sovereignty, and jurisdiction through structured system architecture. This doctrine is factual, educational, and grounded in documented history.

---

I. My Foundational Understanding

Through my study of historical records, state laws, treaties, and administrative practices, I reached a clear conclusion:

Two coordinated systems were used in the United States to dismantle Indigenous nations:
(1) Territorial Usurpation and (2) Identity Usurpation.

These systems operated together.
One removed the land.
The other removed the people on paper.

My work is focused on reversing both.

---

II. Territorial Usurpation

Territorial usurpation refers to the unlawful extension of state authority over sovereign Indigenous nations. This included:

• forced treaties
• removal policies
• state laws overriding tribal jurisdiction
• squatter invasions
• nullification of treaty protections
• the seizure of Chickasaw, Choctaw, Creek, Seminole, Wampanoag, and other Indigenous lands


The pattern was consistent:

1. Declare Indigenous land “vacant,” “unused,” or “needed for settlement.”
2. Extend state law over sovereign territory without consent.
3. Apply pressure to force treaties under unequal conditions.
4. Remove the Nation from its homeland.
5. Open the land to white settlers.


This is what the Chickasaw leaders correctly identified as usurpation—the illegal taking of authority, land, and jurisdiction.

---

III. Identity Usurpation

After territorial usurpation, the next phase was administrative erasure.

Identity usurpation included:

• the 1924 Virginia Racial Integrity Act
• state laws that eliminated “Indian” as a legal category
• reclassification of Indigenous families as “Colored,” “Negro,” or “Mixed”
• the enforcement of the “one‑drop rule”
• the destruction of tribal identity in state records
• the collapse of multiple Indigenous nations into the “Black” category


This was paper genocide—the administrative destruction of a people’s legal identity.

This process severed families from their tribal lineage and removed their ability to assert treaty rights, land claims, or sovereign status.

---

IV. The Link Between the Two Systems

My conclusion is straightforward:

Many people labeled “Black” today descend from Indigenous nations whose identity was erased by law.

This is supported by:

• census records
• racial integrity laws
• tribal rolls
• removal treaties
• colonial descriptions of Indigenous peoples
• state reclassification practices


This explains the oral history passed down through families, including the statement:

“We were here first.”

It reflects the truth that identity was overwritten, not lost.
