1. Definitions

1.1 Jurisdiction (General)

Authority of a government to investigate, charge, try, and punish a person for violating its criminal laws.

1.2 Indian Country (18 U.S.C. § 1151)

Federal law defines Indian Country as:

• All land within reservation boundaries
• All dependent Indian communities
• All allotments with unextinguished Indian title


1.3 Major Crimes Act (MCA) – 18 U.S.C. § 1153

Federal jurisdiction over a list of major felonies committed by Indians in Indian Country.
Currently 16 offenses (murder, manslaughter, kidnapping, sexual abuse, arson, burglary, etc.).

1.4 General Crimes Act (GCA) – 18 U.S.C. § 1152

Federal jurisdiction over crimes involving Indians and non‑Indians, except:

• Indian‑on‑Indian crimes
• Crimes already punished by the tribe
• Crimes where a treaty grants exclusive tribal jurisdiction


1.5 Public Law 280 (PL‑280) – 18 U.S.C. § 1162

Transfers federal criminal jurisdiction to certain states (including California).
Creates state + tribal concurrent jurisdiction over most crimes involving Indians.

1.6 VAWA 2013 – Special Domestic Violence Criminal Jurisdiction (SDVCJ)

Allows tribes to prosecute non‑Indians for:

• Domestic violence
• Dating violence
• Violations of protection orders


Only if the tribe opts in and meets federal requirements.

1.7 Oliphant v. Suquamish (1978)

Tribes generally cannot prosecute non‑Indians (except under VAWA SDVCJ).

1.8 Duro Fix (1991–1992)

Congress restored tribal authority to prosecute non‑member Indians.

1.9 Tribal Sentencing Limits (ICRA + TLOA)

• Standard limit: 1 year imprisonment + $5,000 fine per offense
• Enhanced TLOA limit: 3 years imprisonment + $15,000 fine per offense
Requires:• Licensed defense counsel
• Trained/licensed judges
• Publicly available laws
• Recorded proceedings

4. Additional Rules

4.1 Double Jeopardy

Federal and tribal prosecutions are not double jeopardy because they are separate sovereigns.

4.2 Federal Jurisdiction of General Applicability

Federal laws that apply everywhere (e.g., federal firearms offenses, drug trafficking, RICO) apply in Indian Country regardless of Indian status.

4.3 Criminal vs. Civil Treatment

Some matters that would be criminal in state/federal court must be treated as civil in tribal court due to:

• Lack of jurisdiction over non‑Indians
• PL‑280 limitations
• Resource constraints
